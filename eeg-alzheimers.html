üìÑ Abstract

Research Overview

This research applies Graph Polynomial Vector Autoregressive (GP-VAR) system identification to resting-state EEG signals for characterizing brain network dynamics in Alzheimer's disease (AD).

EEG data from 128-channel recordings are modeled as graph signals on a structural connectivity network derived from the normalized graph Laplacian. The GP-VAR framework enables computation of transfer functions G(œâ, Œª) that jointly characterize temporal frequency œâ and graph frequency Œª responses, providing a novel biomarker of network-level signal processing in the brain.

Analysis of 66 subjects (35 AD, 31 healthy controls) from the BrainLat consortium reveals that AD patients exhibit significantly elevated transfer function magnitudes across multiple frequency bands, with the largest effect in the gamma band (Cohen's d = 1.08). This finding is interpreted as network hyperexcitability‚Äîpathologically increased gain in signal propagation through brain networks.

Transfer function-derived features achieve 87.3% classification accuracy in distinguishing AD from healthy controls, demonstrating clinical utility as an early diagnostic biomarker.

üí° Key Finding

Network Hyperexcitability in Alzheimer's

Contrary to the traditional "EEG slowing" narrative, our transfer function analysis reveals that AD patients exhibit increased |G(œâ, Œª)|‚Äîthe brain network over-amplifies signals rather than under-processing them.

"The brain in Alzheimer's disease doesn't quiet down‚Äîit becomes hyperexcitable, amplifying signals beyond healthy levels."

What This Means

üß† Healthy Brain
Balanced gain: Network appropriately amplifies and attenuates signals for efficient information processing.
üíú Alzheimer's Brain
Elevated gain: Network over-amplifies signals (hyperexcitability), potentially contributing to cognitive dysfunction and seizure susceptibility.

Limitations & Next Steps

Current Limitations

Single-site data (BrainLat consortium only)
Cross-sectional design (no longitudinal tracking)
Binary classification (AD vs HC, no MCI stage)
Population-average graph (not subject-specific DTI)
Linear model (nonlinear dynamics approximated)
Medication effects not fully controlled
